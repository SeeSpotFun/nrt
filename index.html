Research

Smoking While on the Patch: Cardiovascular Safety of NRT

Concerns about cardiovascular risk with NRT patches have been addressed in high-quality research: a population-based, case-control study of 3,643 high-risk patients showed NO association between nicotine patch use and first myocardial infarction (heart attack).
Meta-analyses of 34 randomized controlled trials showed no increase in risk of MI, stroke, or arrhythmia with NRT compared to placebo.
Crude event rates: 0.3–3% (nicotine) vs 0–2% (placebo).

Find evidence at: PMC3549329 (meta-analysis details) | CAMH: Smoking While on the Patch

Evidence for Multiple Patch and Forms of NRT

Research and clinical guidelines strongly support using two or more patches, and combination NRT forms (patch plus gum/lozenges), for very heavy smokers.
Two 21mg patches deliver nicotine blood levels similar to smoking 40-60 cigarettes per day.

  • UK NCSCT: See page 5, “Options for very dependent smokers”
  • Winnipeg Health: "The dose of nicotine replacement should be based on typical cigarettes per day and titrated to effect."
    Medication Recommendations, p. 9
  • Cochrane Library review: Authors’ conclusions

Dosing: Maximum Dose and Clinical Protocols

Clinical guidelines support using up to 4 x 21mg patches (84mg) per day for very heavy smokers.
CAMH: Maximal Dose of Nicotine Patch section
Algorithm: Pharmacotherapy Algorithm, p. 1 (“Maximum is 84mg”)
Dosing charts: CCSME Dosing Chart, Maine CTI Guide, p. 1

Treatment Duration of NRT: Long-term Safety and Efficacy

Extended use (24+ weeks) of NRT is safe and more effective than 8–12 week therapy, though benefits level off with even longer use.
24-week treatment: 27% quit rate (vs 22% at 8 weeks), with excellent safety.
Source: PMC4410859 conclusion

Combined NRT Tips & Tricks

Recommended Therapy

Combined NRT means using both a long-acting patch (for stable nicotine levels) and a short-acting method (for rapid symptom relief).
How to Use: Use a daily patch + add lozenge or gum (or even a nicotine inhaler) as needed. Adjust your short-acting method based on breakthrough cravings or symptoms.

  • Patch: Delivers steady nicotine for 20–24 hours (slow onset, up to 2 hrs). Apply daily, same time, on clean, dry, hairless skin (upper trunk or arm); rotate sites each day and avoid moisturizers.
  • Gum: Chew a piece several times until you taste pepper/spice, then “park” it between your gum and cheek. Repeat chewing/parking as needed. Don’t eat/drink 15 minutes before or after; total use: about 30 minutes per piece.
  • Lozenge: Place between gum and cheek, let dissolve slowly, and move side to side. Do not chew or swallow. Avoid eating/drinking 15 minutes before, during, or after use (especially coffee/colas).

Adjust your short-acting NRT up or down as needed for control. Combining products is proven to increase quit (and comfort) rates!

Dealing with Hand-to-Mouth Habits When Quitting

  • Sugar-free lollipops, hard candy, or gum: Satisfies oral cravings and keeps your hands busy. Dum Dum suckers work great.
  • Flavored toothpicks or chew sticks: Try cinnamon, mint, or plain for oral and hand activity.
  • Cut straw or pen: Use a straw or pen to “puff” or handle.
  • Crunchy raw veggies: Carrot, celery, or cucumber sticks keep your mouth and hands active.
  • Drink through a straw: Sipping water or herbal tea gives you both hand and lip motion.
  • Fidget gadgets: Stress balls or fidget cubes help hands stay busy during cravings.

Tip: Try several options and use them throughout the day! Most people find these urges fade after a few weeks, but support tricks make it much easier.
